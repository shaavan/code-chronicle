# Weekly Log 21: Terms That Hold

> 21 May 2026 – 27 May 2026

## Overview

This week stayed inside the currency work, but the branch kept getting less
interested in sounding complete and more interested in naming who is actually
allowed to know what.

I started by taking exchange-rate authority away from LDK itself. That changed
the shape of the whole feature immediately. Once conversion became an
application-supplied dependency and the result became an accepted range instead
of one exact quote, the rest of the work had to stop leaning on implied
bitcoin-first assumptions. From there the week turned into a longer pass on
ownership. `InvoiceRequest` needed to separate an amount the payer explicitly
names from one the payee may still have to derive from the offer. Invoice
creation needed to treat the converted amount as a real floor. Invoice
verification needed to treat the acceptable converted amount as a real
ceiling. By the end of the week, the useful change was that the flow read less
like currency support threaded into a millisatoshi path and more like one
bounded contract from offer to payment.

## Highlights

### Letting the application own conversion truth

- Reworked the `currency-99` branch so `CurrencyConversion` becomes an
  application-supplied dependency instead of an exchange-rate authority
  invented inside LDK. ([Branch][Currency])

- Changed the conversion contract to return an accepted millisatoshi range,
  which let currency-priced offers carry uncertainty honestly instead of
  flattening it into a fake exact quote.

- Threaded that conversion state through `ChannelManager`, offer and invoice
  construction, plus the surrounding tests and fuzz scaffolding, so
  currency-denominated amounts could move through normal offer flows instead
  of living as a mostly isolated type feature.

### Separating request intent from payee-side resolution

- Split `InvoiceRequest` amount handling into two different things: the
  millisatoshi amount a payer explicitly commits to, and the payable amount
  that can still be derived later from the offer, quantity, and conversion
  rules.

- Relaxed currency-offer request handling so a payer can ask for an invoice
  without already pinning one exact msat total, leaving final amount
  resolution with the payee side that actually owns the offer price.

- Reworked invoice creation to derive currency-priced totals through the
  conversion interface when needed, then made the converted offer amount the
  floor for what an issued invoice is allowed to charge, including
  quantity-scaled requests.

### Giving the payer and payee different edges of the same range

- Added payer-side invoice verification for currency-denominated offers, using
  the acceptable converted ceiling to reject invoices that come back above the
  range the offer allows.

- Split failure cases more cleanly between invoices that are too high and
  invoices that cannot be verified honestly because conversion support is
  unavailable at verification time.

- Closed the remaining gaps between payee-side invoice construction and
  payer-side verification on `currency-100` and `currency-101`, then carried
  the same rules through adjacent invoice paths and end-to-end tests so static
  and ordinary handling do not drift apart.

## Reflections

What kept improving the work this week was letting the two sides of the
payment stop pretending they share one perfect number.

At the start of the branch, currency support still had too much of the old
bitcoin shape in it. The code could often produce a result, but it was still
too willing to act as if there must be one exact millisatoshi answer available
early, and as if LDK itself could stand behind the conversion basis. Returning
an accepted range instead of one quote fixed more than the API. It forced the
rest of the stack to admit where the uncertainty actually lives and who is
responsible for carrying it.

That made the ownership lines much easier to see. The payer can describe
intent, or leave the final msat amount open. The payee can resolve that open
amount, but only by honoring the converted minimum implied by the offer. The
payer does not need to recreate the payee's exact calculation later. It only
needs to check whether the returned invoice still lands under the acceptable
ceiling. Once those edges held, a lot of smaller decisions stopped feeling
arbitrary. Underpriced requests had to fail plainly. Overquoted invoices had
to fail plainly. Unsupported conversion during verification had to stay
separate from an invoice that is simply too expensive.

By the end of the week, the branch felt less like a pile of compatible local
fixes and more like terms both sides can actually enforce. That feels like the
first version of the currency work I could imagine merging without having to
quietly explain the missing pieces around it.
