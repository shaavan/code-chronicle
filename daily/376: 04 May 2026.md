# Log 376: Ground I Could Use

> 04 May 2026

## What I worked on

- [Continued] the BOLT 12 recurrence work on the `rust-recurrence` branch by
  reconciling the invoice-side recurrence stack with the current draft spec and
  the separate continuation-token idea before building further on top of it.
- Resolved the invoice recurrence cherry-pick conflict, finished the pick
  cleanly, and got the branch compiling again with the recurrence pieces back
  in one place.
- Audited the new invoice recurrence path after the build was green and fixed
  mismatches around `invoice_recurrence_basetime` encoding and parsing,
  recurring invoices that were not consistently requiring explicit offer
  basetimes, and refund parse failures surfacing under the wrong semantic
  error.
- Derived and populated recurrence basetime during invoice construction where
  the value is locally knowable, tightened the recurrence validation path, and
  corrected the refund-side error mapping so the failure modes now line up with
  what the fields actually mean.
- Reworked the surrounding documentation and feature commit message so the code
  comments stop mixing internal design notes with user-facing behavior, and the
  commit itself describes the slice of recurrence support that is really there.

## Reflection

Today was less about adding recurrence support than deciding what already
counted as real support and what was still only attached to it.

Before I could trust the invoice-side work again, I had to re-establish the
boundary. The recurrence draft already says some concrete things about invoice
state. The continuation-token idea says others, but only speculatively. Those
two had started sitting too close together in my head, and the cherry-pick did
not become safe until I separated them again. Once that line was clear, the
rest of the review got much more concrete.

The useful part of the day came after the branch compiled. A few pieces were
technically present but still not agreeing on what the recurrence basetime
meant or when it had to exist. Writer and parser behavior were not fully lined
up, recurring invoices were being too loose about explicit offer basetimes, and
the refund-side parse path was reporting one failure under the name of another.
None of that looked dramatic, but all of it would have made the stack harder to
build on because the surface was claiming more precision than it had earned.

What felt better by the end was not just that the code was green again. The
invoice recurrence layer now says more plainly what belongs to upstream
recurrence support and what still belongs to later speculative work. That made
the next step easier to hold. I was no longer stacking token work on top of a
feature branch I merely had compiling. I had something narrow enough to trust.
