# Log 368: What It Can Carry

> 22 April 2026

## What I worked on

- Took yesterday's proposal draft back apart so I could decide what it was
  actually trying to claim and what did not belong in that claim yet.
- Rebuilt the draft around one concrete thesis: making BOLT12 deployable in
  LDK, then organized the case around four production gaps that still block
  that path.
- Rewrote the sections on async payments, onion-message spam resistance, custom
  workflows, and recurring payments so each one led with operational value and
  the implementation pressure behind it.
- Tightened the boundary between specification and implementation, especially
  around custom data and node/server surfaces, so the proposal stopped implying
  more certainty or scope than the work can currently support.
- Compressed the supporting sections until they worked in service of the main
  argument instead of competing with it.

## Reflection

Yesterday's draft had direction, but it was still carrying too much at once.
Too many parts of it were trying to prove usefulness in parallel, which made
the whole document feel broader and less settled than the work beneath it
really is.

The day became about deciding what the document could honestly ask to be judged
on. Once that answer narrowed to making BOLT12 deployable in LDK, the rest of
the editing started to behave differently. The job was no longer to preserve
everything that sounded promising. It was to keep only the work that makes that
claim concrete, and to cut or soften anything that leaned on possibility more
than demonstrated direction.

That changed the four main sections in a useful way. They stopped reading like
adjacent ideas I wanted to include and started reading like connected parts of
one implementation story. Async payments, onion-message abuse resistance,
application-level control, and recurrence each mattered for a different reason,
but they now pointed toward the same deployment surface instead of pulling the
document outward.

The most important edits were the ones that reduced ambition on the page. Being
more careful about the spec-versus-implementation boundary, and about where
node or server work is actually required, made the proposal feel firmer rather
than smaller. By the end, it was saying less, but it was finally saying
something I could stand behind without qualification.
