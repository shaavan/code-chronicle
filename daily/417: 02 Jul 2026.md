# Log 417: Where the Amount Belongs

> 02 July 2026

## What I worked on

- [Continued] the currency-payment branch by returning `invoice_error`
  responses for unverifiable, insufficient, and excessive BOLT 12 invoice
  amounts instead of leaving them as silent outcomes.
- Separated invoice authentication from payment-policy checks after an initial
  move into `ChannelManager` still left amount conversion at the wrong layer.
- Passed currency conversion into outbound payment handling, where amounts are
  validated before payment state is created and mapped into public
  `Bolt12PaymentError` variants.
- Updated manual invoice handling to defer amount rejection until payment is
  attempted, and documented that payable requests select the lower bound of an
  accepted conversion range.
- Added focused coverage for unsupported conversion and excessive amounts,
  including assertions that rejection creates no payment state or failure
  events; targeted compilation passes, while the new tests remain unrun.

## Reflection

The first change today was local. Three amount failures already had enough
information to explain why an invoice could not be paid, yet the payment path
treated them as silent outcomes. Routing them through the nearby
`invoice_error` handling made the failure visible without adding another error
shape.

Following those errors exposed a less local problem. Invoice verification had
accumulated two jobs: authenticating payer metadata and deciding whether the
requested amount was payable under a currency conversion policy. Moving the
amount check into `ChannelManager` looked reasonable at first, but it only
moved the mixed responsibility one layer down. The semantic decision still sat
outside the code that creates and owns an outbound payment.

The useful boundary turned out to be the moment before payment state exists.
The invoice type still validates its raw currency range, `ChannelManager`
authenticates the invoice, and outbound payment handling applies the converter
and maps the result into payment errors. Unsupported conversion and excessive
amounts now fail before they can leave state or failure events behind. Manual
invoice handling follows the same path instead of rejecting an invoice merely
because it was received.

This also made the lower end of an accepted range easier to describe. A
payable request chooses the smallest converted amount the payee accepts. That
rule belongs beside payment construction for the same reason the rejection
does: it decides what will be paid. Invoice authentication remains earlier in
the path.

One API edge remains awkward. Passing a converter through a separate build
method keeps offer builders from becoming generic, but that method still needs
a public shape the bindings can expose. I left that part open. The ownership of
the payment decision now holds; the surface around it still needs work.
