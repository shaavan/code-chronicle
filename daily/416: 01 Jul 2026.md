# Log 416: What the Limit Leaves Behind

> 01 July 2026

## What I worked on

- [Continued] the static-invoice server configuration branch by enforcing each
  authenticated allowance against incoming offer slot numbers.
- Made recipients size and persist the active portion of their static-invoice
  cache from the allowance returned by the server.
- Defined reduced-limit behavior so offers above the new limit stop being
  returned or refreshed but remain stored until their published details expire.
- Preserved compatibility by encoding the configured allowance with new caches
  and deriving it from the offer vector when reading legacy caches.
- Added focused coverage for custom limits, zero and out-of-range values,
  persistence, legacy decoding, and optional `OfferPaths` serialization, then
  passed the full `lightning` test suite.

## Reflection

Yesterday established that the server chooses how many static invoices it is
willing to persist. Today made that number control the system. The server now
rejects slot numbers outside its authenticated range, the recipient activates
only that many cache entries, and the persisted value prevents a restart from
quietly restoring ten.

Reducing an existing allowance was where the number stopped behaving like an
ordinary capacity setting. Some of the offers above the new limit may already
have reached users. Removing them from storage immediately would make those
published payment details fail before their own expiry, even though the server
had accepted them under its previous configuration.

The cache now separates active capacity from the lifetime of material already
published. Entries above a reduced limit are no longer returned or refreshed,
so they cannot keep consuming active slots indefinitely. They remain available
until expiry so outstanding offers still resolve. Raising the limit is easier:
the newly available slots can become active immediately.

Persisted state needed the same distinction without making old caches
unreadable. New encodings carry the allowance directly. Legacy caches recover
it from the length of their fixed-size offer vector, while an empty legacy
cache falls back to ten because it contains no other evidence of its intended
size. The compatibility rule follows what the old data can prove instead of
assigning every historical cache the new configuration.

The focused cases now cover both sides of the boundary, and the full
`lightning` suite passes. Fuzz and bindings checks still remain. Changing the
limit now controls what the recipient maintains next without pretending that
previously published offers vanished at the configuration boundary.
