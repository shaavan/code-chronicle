## Reflection

The first full-suite run left 27 failures spread across payment flows that did
not look closely related. They shared one assumption: tests built their
expectations from fixed dummy-hop parameters while the router now sampled each
path independently. Received amounts, sender fees, CLTV requirements, and route
costs could all move under the assertions.

Making test builds deterministic removed that noise without weakening the
production behavior. The placement of the construction mattered. Keeping it
inside each path-building branch means production still samples each path on
its own; the test configuration changes the values, not the ownership or
lifetime of the policy array.

The next run reduced the failures to 17 and exposed a smaller accounting gap.
Three 1-ppm policies are advertised conservatively as 5 ppm. The sender pays
against that aggregate, but the dummy hops each derive an outgoing amount from
their own policy. On a 10,000,000-msat payment, one calculation adds 50 msat
while the other retains 30 msat. The remaining 20 msat reaches the recipient.
The aggregate is safe for routing, but integer rounding means it cannot be
reversed into the exact per-hop result.

Once that distinction was explicit, the test changes stopped looking like a
collection of adjusted constants. Small payments remain unchanged because both
calculations round to zero. The MPP cases produce their own gaps of 32 and 242
msat, which belong in both recipient and sender accounting. Successive runs
eventually narrowed the suite to those two omitted sender assertions before the
last corrections landed.

The useful part of today was making the tests describe both views of the fee.
The router needs the conservative advertised policy; forwarding applies the
individual policies. Holding those as separate calculations made the failures
predictable, and left the comments explaining why the amounts differ instead
of merely recording what each assertion expects.
