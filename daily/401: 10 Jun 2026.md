# Log 401: Where the Count Belongs

> 10 June 2026

## What I worked on

- [Continued] the dummy-hop fee-accounting work on the `dummy-follow-22`
  branch after a discussion that changed where the solution should live.
- Reframed the problem away from receive-side onion peeling, where dummy hops
  intentionally lose local context once each peeled message is queued for the
  next cycle.
- Worked through a design where expected dummy-hop fees are computed earlier,
  at blinded-path construction time, while the invoice amount is already known.
- Used that earlier construction point to carry the accumulated fee total in
  the final receive TLVs, so the receiver can report it in the final payment
  summary without adding a payment identifier to dummy hops.
- Started implementing the new direction from the path-creation side instead of
  trying to recover state later in the receive flow.

## Reflection

This day helped the dummy-hop fee problem stop feeling like something I had to
outsmart inside the receive path.

The difficult part was never just the accounting. It was that the existing
flow is built to forget exactly the kind of local history I was trying to rely
on. Once a dummy layer is peeled, the next onion message goes back through the
same queued machinery as everything else. That uniformity is part of what
makes the design hold up, so every attempt to attach special receive-side state
to it felt like I was pushing against the shape of the system.

What changed today was realizing the count did not need to be recovered there
at all. By the time blinded payment paths are created, the invoice amount is
already known, which means the expected dummy-hop fees can be computed before
any peeling begins. From there, storing that total in the final receive TLVs
felt much more natural. The receiver gets the number at exactly the point where
it needs to produce the final payment summary, without teaching dummy hops a
new identifier or making their processing path more distinguishable.

I liked this partly because it solved the immediate problem, but mostly because
it stopped asking the receive side to remember something it was designed to let
go.
