# Log 392: The Other Edge

> 26 May 2026

## What I worked on

- [Continued] the BOLT 12 currency work on the `currency-100` branch by
  tightening payer-side invoice verification for currency-denominated offers
  so returned invoices are checked against an explicit converted ceiling
  instead of only passing through shared helper behavior.
- Rejected overquoted invoices directly when the payee comes back above the
  acceptable bound implied by the offer, including quantity-scaled requests
  where both the payable amount and the overcharge tolerance need to grow in
  step.
- Split verification failures more cleanly between invoices that are too high
  and invoices that cannot be verified honestly at all because conversion
  support is unavailable at verification time.
- Added focused coverage for overquoted invoices, unsupported conversion
  during verification, and quantity-scaled currency flows so the payer-side
  contract is visible in behavior instead of only inferred from the payee-side
  rules.

## Reflection

Yesterday made the minimum settlement plain on the payee's side. Today was
about giving that boundary an answer on the other end.

Once invoice creation had a real floor, the remaining risk was that the payer
side could still behave as if any returned quote was acceptable so long as it
looked structurally valid. That left too much of the pricing rule implied. The
better version was narrower. The payer does not need to recreate the payee's
decision. It only needs to know whether the invoice still lands inside the
range the offer allows, and whether that check can be made honestly at all.

That made the failure modes much easier to trust. An invoice that comes back
too high is one kind of problem. An invoice that cannot be verified because
the conversion basis is unavailable is another. Keeping those separate stopped
the validation path from sounding more certain than the inputs really were.
Carrying the same rule through quantity-scaled requests mattered for the same
reason. The bounds only mean anything if they keep their shape once the amount
stops being singular.

By the end of the day the feature felt less like a permissive path with some
helpful checks around it. The payee now owns the floor, the payer owns the
ceiling, and the currency flow reads more like a contract with two live sides
than a bitcoin path with conversion support threaded through it.
