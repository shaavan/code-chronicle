# Weekly Log 24: What Has To Survive

> 09 June 2026 – 16 June 2026

## Overview

This week kept returning to a narrower question than the surface work first
suggested: once a value, a question, or a piece of local state is introduced,
what part of the system is actually responsible for carrying it the rest of
the way.

That question shaped the currency work first. The offer flow already existed,
but too much still depended on repeated conversion, raw fields, or late checks
that made later code responsible for facts that should have been settled
earlier. The dummy-hop work pushed the same pressure through a different part
of the stack, where fee accounting only became trustworthy once it could stay
attached to receiver-side state without making dummy hops look more distinct.
In the middle of that, the recurrence writeup changed form as well. Instead of
arriving as a finished redesign, it became a sharper set of questions that
other contributors could actually pick up and answer from the same place.

By the end of the stretch, the useful progress was not just that these threads
moved forward. It was that each of them got stricter about what had to survive
movement and where that continuity should live.

## Highlights

### Tightening the currency path around one committed amount

- Reworked the exchange-rate model on the `currency-108` branch so common
  rates can be represented directly, tolerances are expressed in basis points,
  and impossible accepted bounds are rejected at construction time instead of
  being left for later call sites to rediscover.

- Moved more of the currency flow behind validated accessors and checked
  construction, which made offer conversion and test utilities depend less on
  raw fields and more on a narrower API that already knows its bounds hold.

- Continued that cleanup on `currency-110` by making invoice-response paths
  resolve one concrete `amount_msats` before they branch, so invoice building,
  inbound payment registration, and blinded-path selection all follow the same
  earlier conversion decision. ([Currency][currency-110])

### Carrying dummy-hop accounting without making it stand out

- Reframed dummy-hop fee accounting around the point where the receiver
  actually has enough information to compute and preserve it, instead of trying
  to recover lost local context from the queued receive path afterward.
  ([Branch][dummy-follow-22])

- Implemented receiver-side accumulation through both recursive local peeling
  and `ChannelManager`'s deferred dummy-hop flow by storing skim state
  alongside queued work, then surfaced the total as
  `dummy_skimmed_fees_msat` in `PaymentClaimable` and `PaymentClaimed`.

- Extended serialization and test helpers so that accounting survives replay,
  restart, and MPP aggregation, while keeping assertions opt-in for tests that
  actually care about dummy-hop skim.

- Tightened privacy camouflage around relay parameters by rounding real-hop
  fees into coarser buckets and letting dummy relay TLVs borrow more ordinary
  nearby profiles instead of carrying exact or obviously synthetic defaults.

### Turning the recurrence redesign into a better discussion

- Reworked the recurrence-spec writeup after mentor feedback so it no longer
  arrives as a fully argued replacement design, and instead puts sharper
  pressure on the current model through a set of focused questions.

- Tightened those questions around the parts of recurrence that still ask too
  much local interpretation from implementers, preserve too much continuity
  across repeated payments, or leave too much room for inconsistent behavior
  between implementations.

- Sent the revised draft back through review in a form that feels easier to
  discuss with the wider community, because the document now makes the present
  pressure legible before trying to settle the redesign underneath it.

## Reflections

What I liked about this stretch was how often the work improved once I stopped
letting later code or later discussion carry facts that should have been fixed
earlier.

The currency branch made that especially concrete. The feature was already
there in broad shape, which made the remaining weaknesses easier to see. They
were not missing pieces so much as repeated chances for the same number to be
re-derived, rechecked, or handled too casually. Once the model got stricter
about validated bounds, and once invoice construction chose one converted
amount early enough, the rest of the path stopped feeling like separate pieces
trying to remain consistent by discipline alone.

The dummy-hop work had a similar effect, but under more architectural
pressure. That path is deliberately built to shed local context as it moves
through queued peeling, and trying to sneak special memory back into it kept
feeling wrong for good reason. The better version was not to outsmart the
queue. It was to let the accounting travel as receiver-owned state long enough
to reach the final event surface, while also making sure the surrounding relay
parameters still pass for ordinary traffic. That combination made the work
feel closer to real privacy engineering than to a local bookkeeping patch.

The recurrence document changed for the same reason in a different medium.
When the writeup arrived as a replacement plan, too much of the real argument
was still hidden inside my own framing. Turning it into questions forced the
pressure to survive without that scaffolding. That left the whole week feeling
more exact about one thing: progress held up best when the important state,
the important amount, or the important question was allowed to stay attached
to the place that could honestly own it.
