# Weekly Log 25: The Promise at the Edge

> 18 June 2026 – 20 June 2026

## Overview

This week stayed inside the dummy-hop follow-up, but the work changed shape as
the tests got closer to passing. It began with an attempt to make the shared
blinded-payment helpers understand the new fee movement. That made the first
failures easier to express, then gradually asked the harness to carry its own
account of behavior the runtime already knew.

Working through the full test fallout by failure class exposed that pressure.
Some failures were stale expectations around forwarded fees, CLTV timing, or
logging. Others came from bounded upward rounding. The last async MPP failure
was different: it showed that the blinded path could advertise more capacity
than the payee could accept once dummy-hop fees were added above the forwarded
amount.

By the end of the stretch, the accounting had moved back into the immediate and
deferred payment-processing paths that own it. The helpers became narrower
again, and the final red test turned into a correction to the limit exposed by
the path rather than another exception in the harness.

## Highlights

### Following fee accounting through the tests

- Extended shared blinded-payment helpers to consume
  `dummy_skimmed_fees_msat` from `PaymentClaimed`, allowing sent-side
  assertions to include dummy-hop effects without hardcoded values at every
  call site.

- Added receive-side reconstruction and extra-fee helpers, then documented the
  integer-rounding behavior that prevents old exact inverse assumptions from
  holding across the path.

- Kept this work separate from the earlier runtime changes so the test
  framework could be evaluated on its own as the proof of the feature.

### Sorting failures by what they were actually saying

- Worked through the remaining `lightning` test failures as groups, restoring
  forwarded-event fee expectations and correcting an async payment case that
  omitted the first blinded-tail forwarding fee.

- Updated held-HTLC timeout coverage for the longer dummy-hop CLTV path and
  removed a stale static-invoice log assertion whose underlying invalid-onion
  failure was already correct.

- Reduced the remaining breakage to tests whose exact amount and fee
  assumptions no longer accounted for bucketed relay parameters and bounded
  upward rounding.

### Putting ownership back in the payment path

- Pulled amount reconstruction out of the shared helpers and preserved dummy
  skim in both immediate onion peeling and deferred `update_add_htlc` decoding,
  giving both receive paths the same accounting without a parallel model in
  tests.

- Kept rounding differences local to the assertions where they appear instead
  of broadening shared helpers around them.

- Traced the final async MPP regression to the advertised inbound HTLC maximum,
  which did not leave room for fees added by dummy hops, then applied
  `amt_to_forward_msat` through each dummy hop before publishing that limit.
  ([Branch][dummy-follow-29])

## Reflections

The useful part of this week was watching a reasonable testing approach reach
its limit. Feeding `PaymentClaimed` data into the helpers initially gave the
suite a common way to understand dummy-hop fees. As more amount flow was
reconstructed there, though, the helpers stopped describing observable
behavior and started maintaining a second version of the feature.

Grouping the failures made that easier to see. Forwarded-fee expectations,
timeout heights, and stale logs could each be repaired where their assumptions
had changed. Rounding differences were also narrow once they were treated as
the deliberate cost of avoiding underfunded paths. None of those needed a
larger accounting model. They needed assertions that matched the specific
behavior being checked.

The final MPP regression was valuable because it did not fit that pattern. The
test kept failing when its payment amount or channel limit moved, which meant
the boundary itself was wrong. A blinded path was offering the payee's full
inbound capacity even though the sender still had to place dummy-hop fees on
top. Running the limit backwards through those hops made the advertised
maximum describe an amount the channel could really receive.

That left the branch in a better state than teaching the test framework enough
arithmetic to make the suite green. Receive-side accounting now survives both
processing paths, small rounding effects stay visible where they matter, and
the capacity promise at the edge accounts for the fees needed to reach it.
