# Log 364: What Survives

> 14 April 2026

## What I worked on

- [Continued] the recurrence transition work on the new `rust-recurrence`
  branch.
- Re-audited the old payer-recurrence PoC to separate durable protocol surface
  from temporary recurrence-session logic.
- Reviewed `ldk-node` directly to confirm it has the orchestration,
  persistence, and event surface needed to own the recurrence lifecycle.
- Restarted the branch from `upstream/main` so the work could be shaped as
  generic BOLT12 seams instead of more recurrence-specific `ChannelManager`
  growth.

## Reflection

Today was about testing whether yesterday's boundary still held once I put it
back against the code.

It is easy for an architectural split to sound right in conversation and still
fall apart when the actual implementation is spread across two codebases. I
spent the day pressure-testing that split instead of treating it as settled. On
the old payer-recurrence PoC branch, the distinction became much clearer once I
looked at it through that lens. The recurrence primitives and some builder and
signing work still felt like real protocol surface. Most of the rest did not.
The session tracking, continuity rules, expiry handling, and recurrence-facing
APIs all looked increasingly like node-level machinery that had been pulled too
far down into `ChannelManager`.

Reviewing `ldk-node` was the part that made the plan feel earned. The move only
makes sense if the other side is actually ready to absorb it. Seeing the
payment surface, persistence model, event plumbing, and central `Node` layer in
place made the transition feel less like cleanup and more like a believable
reassignment of ownership.

That changed the branch itself. Instead of continuing from the PoC line and
trying to carve it down in place, I started over from `upstream/main`. That
reset felt important because it removed the old assumptions from the work and
forced every reintroduced piece to justify itself. By the end of the day, the
question was no longer what I could preserve from the PoC. It was what truly
needed to survive the move.
