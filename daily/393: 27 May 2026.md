# Log 393: Two-Sided Terms

> 27 May 2026

## What I worked on

- [Continued] the BOLT 12 currency work on the `currency-101` branch by
  closing the remaining gaps between payee-side invoice construction and
  payer-side verification so currency-priced offers behave like one bounded
  payment path instead of a set of local fixes.
- Made invoice construction derive the payable millisatoshi amount from the
  offer when the final amount is still open, and treated the converted offer
  amount as the floor for what the payee is allowed to issue.
- Matched that with payer-side verification against the acceptable converted
  ceiling, including quantity-scaled requests where both the payable amount
  and the tolerance range need to grow together.
- Tightened surrounding failure paths so unsupported conversions and
  out-of-bounds amounts surface as explicit errors, then carried the same
  rules through adjacent invoice paths and end-to-end tests so static and
  ordinary handling do not drift apart.

## Reflection

This day felt like finishing negotiation and finally writing down terms.

Most of the individual currency pieces already existed by this point, but they
still read too much like isolated corrections. The payee could do the right
thing in one place, the payer could reject the wrong thing in another, and the
shape of the feature still depended on whether someone remembered how those
helpers were meant to line up. What made the work feel complete was letting the
two sides own different edges of the same range. The payee resolves the open
amount by honoring the converted minimum. The payer accepts the returned
invoice only if it still lands under the acceptable ceiling. Same price model,
different obligations.

I liked that asymmetry more the longer I sat with it. It stopped the currency
path from feeling like bitcoin logic with conversion threaded through the side.
The payee does not need to guess what the payer will tolerate, and the payer
does not need to recreate the payee's calculation from the same position. Each
side only has to enforce the boundary it actually owns. Once that became
explicit, the rest of the hardening work started to feel necessary rather than
decorative. Unsupported conversions had to fail plainly. Quantity scaling had
to preserve the same bounds instead of quietly changing their meaning. The
neighboring invoice paths had to carry the same rules or the model would split
again.

By the end, the currency flow no longer felt like a promising branch with good
local behavior. It read like one contract from offer to payment, with enough
tests around it that the boundaries are visible in behavior instead of only in
intent.
